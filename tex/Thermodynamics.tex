  \large A Conditional Variational Result and the Open Thermodynamic Bridge}

  \begin{abstract}
    We separate a conditional variational result from a thermodynamic interpretation that is not yet established.
    Let $\Spi^{\mathrm{ren}}[g]=\half\log\det'(\Ag/\mu^2)$ be the renormalized projective spectral functional.
    Assuming a renormalized infrared metric response and an effective matter functional $W_\Pi[g,\psi]$,
    stationarity of $\Gamma_\Pi=\Spi^{\mathrm{ren}}-W_\Pi$ yields
    $G_{\mu\nu}+\Lambda_{\mathrm{ren}}g_{\mu\nu}=8\pi G_N T^{(\Pi)}_{\mu\nu}$
    at leading derivative order.
    The coefficient follows directly from the ratio between the matter variation factor $1/2$ and the geometric
    coefficient $(16\pi G_N)^{-1}$.

    We then audit the diagonal heat-kernel quantity
    $u(x;t)=-\partial_t\log K(x,x;t)$.
    For a four-dimensional minimal scalar Laplacian it has dimension $L^{-2}$ and expansion
    $u=2/t-R/6+O(t\mathcal{R}^2,t\nabla^2R)$.
    Its normalized form $b(x;t)=tu(x;t)/2=1-tR/12+\cdots$ is a dimensionless spectral response.
    Neither quantity is a physical temperature, an inverse temperature, or an energy density without an additional
    calibration map.
    Consequently no local first law follows from the heat kernel alone.
    A thermodynamic completion requires an independently defined heat one-form on configuration space, a physical
    energy notion, and an integrability theorem establishing $\delta Q=T\,\dd S$.

    \textbf{Interpretive status.}
    The conditional content is a variational coupling between a renormalized geometric functional and matter.
    Its spectral derivation depends on a regulator and matching prescription that remain to be fixed in the companion
    Gravity paper.
    Reading that stationarity as equilibrium remains a programme-level interpretation, not a result of this paper.
  \end{abstract}

  \noindent\textbf{Keywords:} spectral determinant, heat kernel, induced gravity,
  Einstein equation, matter coupling, variational principle, thermodynamic integrability

  \section{Introduction}
    \label{sec:intro}

    The companion Gravity paper~\cite{Beau2026h} studies the renormalized metric response of the projective
    spectral functional and identifies its leading infrared local term with the Einstein tensor.
    The present paper asks what follows when that geometric functional is coupled to an independently defined
    effective matter functional.

    The answer has two sharply different parts.
    First, a standard joint variational problem conditionally produces the Einstein--matter equation from specified
    renormalized coefficients.
    Second, the diagonal heat kernel supplies a useful local spectral scale, but it does not by itself supply heat,
    temperature, energy, or a first law.
    Keeping these statements separate is necessary for dimensional and variational consistency.

    \subsection{Conditional variational result}

      Let $W_\Pi[g,\psi]$ be an effective matter functional whose metric variation defines
      $T^{(\Pi)}_{\mu\nu}$.
      If the leading infrared variation of $\Spi^{\mathrm{ren}}$ contains specified Einstein and cosmological
      responses, stationarity of $\Spi^{\mathrm{ren}}-W_\Pi$ gives the Einstein equation with a renormalized
      cosmological constant.
      This statement is spectral and variational.
      It does not require a horizon, a heat flux, or a temperature.

    \subsection{Status correction relative to version 1.1}
      \label{subsec:status-correction}

      Version 1.1 used the terms ``local spectral energy density'', ``temperature multiplier'', ``local spectral
      first law'', and ``spectral equilibrium'' for structures that had not been connected to operational
      thermodynamic quantities.
      It also assigned mutually incompatible dimensions to the same multiplier, treated a total energy variation as
      a local density, and replaced a constant variational coupling by a curvature-dependent field without varying
      that field.

      Version 2.0 withdraws the following claims of version 1.1:
      \begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
        \item $-\partial_t\log K(x,x;t)$ is a local energy density or temperature;
        \item the heat kernel establishes a local first law;
        \item compactly supported metric variations turn a constant coupling into a local temperature field;
        \item the Einstein equation derived here is, by that derivation alone, an equilibrium equation of state.
      \end{enumerate}
      The joint-variation identity is retained and restated as conditional on its renormalized geometric input.

    \subsection{Structure of the paper}

      Section~\ref{sec:spectral-locality} defines the dimensionally consistent local spectral response.
      Section~\ref{sec:equilibrium} proves the joint variational result.
      Section~\ref{sec:normalization} isolates the normalization and the role of a possible dimensionless coupling.
      Section~\ref{sec:first-law} states what a genuine thermodynamic completion would require.
      Section~\ref{sec:relation-jacobson} compares neighbouring approaches.
      Section~\ref{sec:discussion} gives limitations and an interpretive outlook.

  \section{Spectral determinant and local spectral response}
    \label{sec:spectral-locality}

    \subsection{Dimensionless renormalized spectral functional}

      Let $(M,g)$ be a smooth four-dimensional Riemannian manifold and let
      $\Ag=-\nabla_g^2$ be a positive minimal scalar Laplacian.
      Since $[\Ag]=L^{-2}$, a determinant requires a reference scale $\mu$.
      We use the renormalized dimensionless functional
      \begin{equation}
        \Spi^{\mathrm{ren}}[g]
        \equiv
        \half\log\det'\!\left(\frac{\Ag}{\mu^2}\right).
        \label{eq:Spi-def}
      \end{equation}
      The prime excludes zero modes.
      The name ``spectral entropy'' is retained as corpus terminology, but no thermodynamic entropy is inferred from
      the determinant in this paper.

    \subsection{Heat-kernel expansion}

      The heat kernel satisfies
      \begin{equation}
        (\partial_t+\Ag)\hk(x,y;t)=0,
        \qquad
        \hk(x,y;0^+)=\delta_g(x,y),
        \label{eq:heat-eq}
      \end{equation}
      with $[t]=L^2$ and $[\hk(x,x;t)]=L^{-4}$.
      In the convention where the universal factor is outside the coefficient series,
      \begin{equation}
        \hk(x,x;t)
        \sim
        \frac{1}{(4\pi t)^2}
        \left(a_0(x)+a_2(x)t+a_4(x)t^2+\cdots\right),
        \qquad t\to0^+.
        \label{eq:hk-expansion}
      \end{equation}
      For the minimal scalar Laplacian,
      \begin{align}
        a_0(x)&=1,
        \label{eq:a0}\\
        a_2(x)&=\frac{1}{6}R(x),
        \label{eq:a2}\\
        a_4(x)&=\frac{1}{360}
        \left(
          12\nabla^2R+5R^2-2R_{\mu\nu}R^{\mu\nu}
          +2R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}R^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
        \right).
        \label{eq:a4}
      \end{align}
      This convention must not include a second factor $(4\pi)^{-2}$ inside $a_4$~\cite{Vassilevich2003,
      Gilkey1995}.

    \subsection{Local spectral response}

      \begin{definition}[Local spectral rate]
        \label{def:u}
        The local spectral rate at diffusion scale $t$ is
        \begin{equation}
          u(x;t)\equiv-\partial_t\log\hk(x,x;t).
          \label{eq:u-def}
        \end{equation}
      \end{definition}

      The logarithm may be understood relative to a fixed reference kernel.
      Such a reference changes the logarithm by a $t$-independent term and therefore does not change $u$.

      \begin{proposition}[Short-time spectral rate]
        \label{prop:u-expansion}
        The local spectral rate has the expansion
        \begin{equation}
          u(x;t)
          =\frac{2}{t}-\frac{1}{6}R(x)
          +O\!\left(t\mathcal{R}^2,t\nabla^2R\right),
          \label{eq:u-expansion}
        \end{equation}
        where $\mathcal{R}^2$ denotes quadratic curvature invariants.
      \end{proposition}

      \begin{proof}
        Taking the logarithm of~\eqref{eq:hk-expansion} gives
        \[
          \log\hk=-2\log(4\pi t)+\frac{1}{6}Rt
          +O\!\left(t^2\mathcal{R}^2,t^2\nabla^2R\right).
        \]
        Differentiation and multiplication by $-1$ give~\eqref{eq:u-expansion}.
      \end{proof}

      Since $[u]=L^{-2}$, $u$ has the dimension of a Laplace eigenvalue.
      It has neither the dimension of energy nor that of temperature in natural units.

    \subsection{Dimensionless curvature response}

      \begin{definition}[Normalized local response]
        \label{def:beta}
        Relative to the flat short-time value $u_0(t)=2/t$, define
        \begin{equation}
          b(x;t)
          \equiv
          \frac{u(x;t)}{u_0(t)}
          =1-\frac{t}{12}R(x)
          +O\!\left(t^2\mathcal{R}^2,t^2\nabla^2R\right).
          \label{eq:beta-def}
        \end{equation}
      \end{definition}

      The field $b$ is dimensionless and covariantly defined once the diffusion scale is specified.
      It measures the leading curvature correction to the local spectral rate.
      It is not a temperature or inverse temperature.

      \begin{remark}[Sign of the correction]
        \label{rem:sign}
        Positive scalar curvature increases $K(x,x;t)$ at order $t$ but decreases its logarithmic decay rate $u$.
        The sign in~\eqref{eq:beta-def} concerns the decay rate, not a reduction of the heat-kernel density itself.
      \end{remark}

      \begin{remark}[Status of a fixed diffusion scale]
        \label{rem:tstar}
        Evaluating $b(x;t)$ at a chosen $t=t_*$ produces a scale-dependent spectral diagnostic.
        The short-time expansion requires $t_*\mathcal{R}\ll1$.
        No cited result currently identifies $t_*$ with an inverse physical temperature or with
        $\ell_{\mathrm{sp}}^2$.
      \end{remark}

  \section{Joint spectral--matter stationarity}
    \label{sec:equilibrium}

    \subsection{Independent matter functional}

      Let $W_\Pi[g,\psi]$ be an effective matter functional on the same four-dimensional manifold.
      We fix the stress-tensor convention by
      \begin{equation}
        \delta W_\Pi[g,\psi]
        =\half\int_M\dd^4x\,\sqrt{g}\,
        T^{(\Pi)}_{\mu\nu}\,\delta g^{\mu\nu}.
        \label{eq:W-variation}
      \end{equation}
      This equation defines a covariant action variation.
      It does not define an energy on a spatial slice.

    \subsection{Infrared geometric input}

      We state the renormalized geometric input as an explicit assumption.
      Its derivation from a spectral regulator and its finite matching prescription are deferred to a corrected
      treatment in Gravity~\cite{Beau2026h}.
      The leading local form is
      \begin{equation}
        \delta\Spi^{\mathrm{ren}}
        =\int_M\dd^4x\,\sqrt{g}\,
        \left[
          \frac{1}{16\pi G_N}G_{\mu\nu}
          +c_\Lambda g_{\mu\nu}
          +H_{\mu\nu}^{(4)}+H_{\mu\nu}^{\mathrm{nonloc}}
        \right]\delta g^{\mu\nu},
        \label{eq:deltaS-EH}
      \end{equation}
      where $H_{\mu\nu}^{(4)}$ contains local four-derivative terms and
      $H_{\mu\nu}^{\mathrm{nonloc}}$ contains finite non-local terms.
      In the weak-curvature infrared regime their contribution is subleading relative to the Einstein term.
      Define the renormalized cosmological constant by
      $\Lambda_{\mathrm{ren}}\equiv16\pi G_Nc_\Lambda$.

    \subsection{Stationarity theorem}

      \begin{definition}[Joint spectral--matter functional]
        \label{def:equilibrium}
        Define
        \begin{equation}
          \Gamma_\Pi[g,\psi]
          \equiv
          \Spi^{\mathrm{ren}}[g]-W_\Pi[g,\psi].
          \label{eq:F-functional}
        \end{equation}
      \end{definition}

      \begin{theorem}[Conditional stationarity]
        \label{thm:einstein}
        Assume~\eqref{eq:W-variation} and~\eqref{eq:deltaS-EH}.
        Work at leading local infrared order.
        Then $\delta\Gamma_\Pi=0$ for every compactly supported metric variation if and only if
        \begin{equation}
          G_{\mu\nu}+\Lambda_{\mathrm{ren}}g_{\mu\nu}
          =8\pi G_N T^{(\Pi)}_{\mu\nu}.
          \label{eq:einstein}
        \end{equation}
      \end{theorem}

      \begin{proof}
        Combining the two independently defined variations gives
        \begin{equation}
          \delta\Gamma_\Pi
          =\int_M\dd^4x\,\sqrt{g}\,
          \left[
            \frac{1}{16\pi G_N}G_{\mu\nu}
            +c_\Lambda g_{\mu\nu}
            -\half T^{(\Pi)}_{\mu\nu}
          \right]\delta g^{\mu\nu}
          +\delta\Gamma_\Pi^{\mathrm{sub}}.
          \label{eq:delta-F}
        \end{equation}
        Neglecting the controlled subleading term $\delta\Gamma_\Pi^{\mathrm{sub}}$ in the stated infrared regime,
        the fundamental lemma of the calculus of variations yields
        \begin{equation}
          \frac{1}{16\pi G_N}G_{\mu\nu}
          +c_\Lambda g_{\mu\nu}
          =\half T^{(\Pi)}_{\mu\nu}.
          \label{eq:EL-pointwise}
        \end{equation}
        Dividing by $(16\pi G_N)^{-1}$ gives~\eqref{eq:einstein}.
      \end{proof}

      The theorem is a conditional joint effective-action statement.
      Setting $c_\Lambda=0$ is an additional renormalization condition, not an infrared approximation.
      Calling its stationarity ``equilibrium'' would add an interpretation not used in the proof.

  \section{Normalization and coupling status}
    \label{sec:normalization}

    \subsection{Correct normalization of the Einstein coefficient}

      The two coefficients entering~\eqref{eq:EL-pointwise} are
      \begin{equation}
        c_{\mathrm{EH}}=(16\pi G_N)^{-1},
        \qquad
        c_T=\half.
        \label{eq:geom-norm}
      \end{equation}
      Therefore the coefficient multiplying $T^{(\Pi)}_{\mu\nu}$ after solving for $G_{\mu\nu}$ is
      \begin{equation}
        \frac{c_T}{c_{\mathrm{EH}}}
        =\frac{\half}{(16\pi G_N)^{-1}}
        =8\pi G_N.
        \label{eq:8piG-factorization}
      \end{equation}
      This is a ratio, not the inverse of the product $c_Tc_{\mathrm{EH}}$.

      \begin{proposition}[Normalization identity]
        \label{prop:normalization}
        With the conventions~\eqref{eq:W-variation} and~\eqref{eq:deltaS-EH}, the stationarity equation has the
        standard matter coefficient $8\pi G_N$ once the renormalized geometric coefficient is supplied.
      \end{proposition}

    \subsection{Optional dimensionless relative coupling}

      One may instead study
      \begin{equation}
        \Gamma_{\Pi,\kappa}=\Spi^{\mathrm{ren}}-\kappa W_\Pi,
        \qquad [\kappa]=1.
        \label{eq:matter-norm}
      \end{equation}
      Its leading stationarity equation is
      \begin{equation}
        G_{\mu\nu}+\Lambda_{\mathrm{ren}}g_{\mu\nu}
        =8\pi G_N\kappa T^{(\Pi)}_{\mu\nu}.
        \label{eq:coupling-renorm}
      \end{equation}
      The constant $\kappa$ is a relative normalization or coupling.
      It is not an inverse temperature and does not parameterize departure from equilibrium.

      \begin{remark}[Single-determinant sector]
        \label{rmk:single-determinant}
        Q12 and Q13~\cite{Beau2026q12,Beau2026q13} investigate whether geometric and gauge terms arise as different
        Seeley--DeWitt orders of one joint determinant.
        If a common normalization is established there, it may select $\kappa$ in that restricted sector.
        This conditional identification does not establish a thermodynamic temperature and is not assumed in
        Theorem~\ref{thm:einstein}.
      \end{remark}

  \section{Why a thermodynamic first law does not yet follow}
    \label{sec:first-law}

    \subsection{Dimensional obstruction}

      In natural units a physical temperature and energy have dimension $L^{-1}$, while inverse temperature has
      dimension $L$.
      The spectral rate $u$ has dimension $L^{-2}$ and the normalized response $b$ is dimensionless.
      No identification among these quantities is dimensionally available without an additional scale and a physical
      calibration law.

      \begin{proposition}[No temperature from the diagonal kernel alone]
        \label{thm:first-law}
        The data $(\Ag,K,t)$ and the definition $u=-\partial_t\log K$ do not determine a physical temperature,
        inverse temperature, heat, or energy density.
      \end{proposition}

      \begin{proof}
        The construction fixes only a Laplace-eigenvalue scale $[u]=L^{-2}$ and its dimensionless normalized response.
        A physical temperature requires both a quantity of dimension $L^{-1}$ and an operational thermal
        interpretation.
        Neither is supplied by the diagonal kernel.
      \end{proof}

    \subsection{Action variation is not an energy variation}

      Equation~\eqref{eq:W-variation} is a four-dimensional action variation.
      An energy on a hypersurface would require a Lorentzian geometry, a time flow, a unit normal, a measure on the
      hypersurface, and control of how all these structures vary with the metric.
      Restricting~\eqref{eq:W-variation} to a spatial slice does not produce such a variation.

      For reference, a genuine local Clausius relation would have the schematic dimensionally homogeneous form
      \begin{equation}
        \delta S
        =\int_\Sigma\beta_{\mathrm{phys}}(x)\,\delta q(x)\,\dd\Sigma,
        \qquad
        [\beta_{\mathrm{phys}}]=L,
        \quad
        [\delta q]=L^{-4}.
        \label{eq:first-law}
      \end{equation}
      No $\beta_{\mathrm{phys}}$ or heat density $\delta q$ is derived here.

    \subsection{Integrability requirement}

      Carath\'{e}odory's formulation makes the existence of entropy an integrability question for a Pfaffian heat
      form~\cite{Caratheodory1909}.
      Roberts recently expressed this structure as a gauge connection on a work bundle~\cite{Roberts2026Heat}.
      If $\xi$ is an independently defined heat one-form and $H=\ker\xi$ the adiabatic connection, local functions
      $T$ and $S$ satisfying
      \begin{equation}
        \xi=T\,\dd S
        \label{eq:delta-E-Pi}
      \end{equation}
      exist precisely under the corresponding local integrability or flatness condition.
      Roberts also relates the result to Jauch's conservation proposal~\cite{Jauch1972,Roberts2026Origin}.

      Cosmochrony does not yet provide the independent one-form $\xi_\Pi$ required to apply this theorem.
      Defining $\xi_\Pi\equiv T\,\dd\Spi$ would make integrability tautological and would not derive a physical heat
      form.

      \begin{remark}[Local and global obstructions]
        \label{rem:identity-nature}
        Even a locally flat adiabatic connection may have non-trivial global holonomy.
        A thermodynamic completion must therefore distinguish local integrability from global equilibrium.
        This thermal connection would also be distinct from the internal gauge connection associated with the
        non-injective projection fibres of Cosmochrony.
      \end{remark}

    \subsection{Why a local curvature multiplier is not generated by local variation}

      Compactly supported variations already yield the pointwise equation~\eqref{eq:EL-pointwise} with the same
      constant coupling $\kappa$.
      They do not replace $\kappa$ by $b(x;t_*)$ or by $u(x;t_*)$.
      Write the matter functional locally as
      $W_\Pi=\int_M\dd^4x\,\sqrt{g}\,\mathcal{L}_\Pi$.
      A candidate functional with a metric-dependent local coupling would instead satisfy schematically
      \begin{equation}
        \delta\!\left(
          \Spi^{\mathrm{ren}}
          -\int_M\dd^4x\,\sqrt{g}\,b[g]\mathcal{L}_\Pi
        \right)
        =\delta\Spi^{\mathrm{ren}}
        -\int_M\dd^4x\,b[g]\,\delta\!\left(\sqrt{g}\mathcal{L}_\Pi\right)
        -\int_M\dd^4x\,\sqrt{g}\mathcal{L}_\Pi\,\delta b[g].
        \label{eq:local-einstein}
      \end{equation}
      The last term cannot be discarded because $b[g]$ depends on $R[g]$.

      \begin{remark}[Normalization is not a gauge transformation]
        \label{rem:gauge}
        Choosing units in which a numerical reference value equals one is harmless, but it does not convert a
        dimensionful field into a dimensionless coupling.
        The invariant normalized object is $b=u/u_0$, whose leading correction is $-tR/12$.
      \end{remark}

  \section{Relation to neighbouring approaches}
    \label{sec:relation-jacobson}

    \subsection{Jacobson's horizon thermodynamics}
      \label{subsec:jacobson}

      Jacobson derives the Einstein equation from the Clausius relation on local Rindler horizons, using horizon area,
      Unruh temperature, matter heat flux, and the Raychaudhuri equation~\cite{Jacobson1995}.
      None of those thermodynamic ingredients appears in Theorem~\ref{thm:einstein}.
      The shared output is the Einstein equation; the premises and logical route are different.

    \subsection{Carath\'{e}odory, Jauch, and Roberts}
      \label{subsec:unruh-analogy}

      Carath\'{e}odory treats entropy and temperature through local integrability of the heat form.
      Jauch seeks their origin in a conservation statement, and Roberts reconstructs that proposal using connection
      geometry~\cite{Caratheodory1909,Jauch1972,Roberts2026Heat,Roberts2026Origin}.
      This line supplies a precise test for a future thermodynamic completion of Cosmochrony.
      It does not identify the thermodynamic work bundle with the internal projection bundle.

    \subsection{Wald entropy}
      \label{subsec:wald}

      Wald identifies black-hole entropy with a Noether charge for diffeomorphism-invariant gravitational
      theories~\cite{Wald1993}.
      The present paper neither introduces a horizon nor evaluates a Noether charge.
      Higher-curvature terms in the spectral determinant may be studied with Wald's formalism after a Lorentzian
      action and a horizon are specified, but no equality of entropies is asserted here.

    \subsection{Quantum relative entropy}
      \label{subsec:relative-entropy}

      Dorau and Much derive semiclassical Einstein equations from quantum relative entropy for quantum fields on a
      horizon-bearing background~\cite{DorauMuch2026}.
      The present primitive is instead a spectral determinant of an admissible operator.
      A relative determinant
      \begin{equation}
        \Spi(g\Vert g_0)
        =\half\log\frac{\det'(\Ag/\mu^2)}{\det'(A_{g_0}/\mu^2)}
        \label{rmk:relative-spi}
      \end{equation}
      is well defined when the determinant ratio is controlled, but it is not thereby an Araki--Uhlmann entropy.

      \begin{remark}[Entropy nomenclature]
        \label{rmk:entropy-nomenclature}
        The spectral functional $\Spi$, the history-branching entropy of the cosmological sector~\cite{Beau2026d},
        and the measure-theoretic projection entropy of the emergence criterion~\cite{Beau2026l} are distinct.
        No thermodynamic identification among them is made here.
      \end{remark}

  \section{Discussion and outlook}
    \label{sec:discussion}

    \subsection{What follows conditionally}
      \label{subsec:summary}

      The local spectral rate has a controlled short-time curvature expansion and a dimensionless normalized response
      $b=1-tR/12+\cdots$.
      Conditional on specified renormalized Einstein and cosmological coefficients, joint stationarity with an
      independently defined matter functional yields the Einstein--matter equation at leading infrared order.
      The numerical matter coefficient $8\pi G_N$ is then the ratio of the two variation coefficients.

    \subsection{What remains open}
      \label{subsec:limitations}

      A physical matter sector $W_\Pi[g,\psi]$ must still be constructed for each projected species.
      The Lorentzian definition of energy and heat must be supplied independently of the Euclidean determinant.
      A thermodynamic bridge requires a heat one-form, an operational temperature calibration, a statistical measure,
      and an integrability analysis.
      Higher-derivative and non-local responses must be retained outside the weak-curvature infrared regime.
      The companion Gravity paper must also separate the proper-time-cutoff origin of an
      $a_2\Lambda_{\mathrm{UV}}^2R$ term from the $a_4$ scale dependence of a zeta-regularized determinant and state
      the matching condition that defines the finite renormalized value of $G_N$.

    \subsection{Interpretive outlook}
      \label{subsec:outlook}

      \textbf{Interpretation, not derived result.}
      Spectral stationarity may eventually be understood as an equilibrium principle if the missing thermodynamic
      structures are constructed and shown to select the same stationary geometries.
      At present the defensible reading is narrower: if the admissible spectral operator and its renormalization
      prescription supply the assumed geometric coefficients, coupling that functional to projected matter
      reproduces the infrared Einstein response.
      This identifies where a thermodynamic interpretation could attach without treating analogy as derivation.
